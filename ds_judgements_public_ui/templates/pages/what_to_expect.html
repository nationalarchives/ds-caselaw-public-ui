About the Find Case Law service

The Find Case Law service provides public access to court judgments and tribunal decisions.

From April 2022, court judgments from the England and Wales High Court, the Court of Appeal, the Supreme Court and tribunal decisions from the Upper Tribunals are being sent to The National Archives to be preserved and made available to the public.

The service only contains judgments and decisions that have been handed down and made public. It does not include settlements made outside of court.

Alpha service

The Find Case Law service is in the very early stages of development, referred to as the ‘Alpha’ phase. This means that this is the first version of the service, which will develop and improve over the next few months and years.

Your feedback will help us develop the service

Our legal basis for archiving and publishing case law

The Public Records Act 1958 provides the legal basis for the Find Case Law service.

Court judgments and tribunal decisions are public records. They fall within the scope of court records, defined by the First Schedule of the Public Records Act 1958. The judgments that are published through the Find Case Law service have been selected for permanent preservation and have been transferred to The National Archives in accordance with Section 3, or have been acquired under Section 2(4)(h).

Judgments and tribunal decisions are being preserved and made available to the public. The Keeper of Public Records at The National Archives is required to take all practicable steps for the preservation of records under his charge under Section 2(3), and to arrange that reasonable facilities are available to the public for inspecting and obtaining copies of those public records in the Public Records Office which fall to be disclosed in accordance with the Freedom of Information Act 2000 under Section 5(3). The Find Case Law service fulfils these statutory duties.

Limited coverage of judgments and decisions

The Find case law service is an Alpha service.

The initial collection of judgments and decisions totals to around 50,000 documents that date back to 2003 for Court Judgments, and to 2016 for Tribunal Decisions.

The collection includes:

  • All judgments and tribunal decisions from the Upper Tribunals, High Court, Court of Appeal and Supreme Court from 19 April 2022
  • First-tier Tribunals (General Regulatory and Tax Chambers)
  • Judgments and decisions provided by the British and Irish Legal Information Institure (BAILII), that have been published under its contract with His Majesty's Courts and Tribunals Service (HMCTS)/Ministry of Justice (MoJ)
  • Judgments provided directly by the Supreme Court and the Upper Tribunals

The collection does not include judgments from:

  • Crown courts
  • County courts
  • Magistrates' courts
  • United Kingdom Competition Appeals Tribunal
  • United Kingdom Employment Tribunal
  • First-tier Tribunals
    • Health, Education and Social Care Chamber
    • Immigration and Asylum Chamber
    • Property Chamber
    • Social Entitlement Chamber
    • War Pensions and Armed Forces Compensation Chamber

To widen the coverage of the service, we have bought digital copies of other judgments and decisions. We are currently working through these judgments and decisions to get them ready for publication and expand our collection.

Initially only judgments and decisions with neutral citations will be available. We are working to assign identifiers to judgments and decisions without neutral citations.

Judgments and decisions as data

We have made judgments and decisions available as data in order to facilitate transparency of the administration of justice and enable innovation. We have adopted the international standard legal document mark-up language (LegalDocML).

Judgments and decisions are sent to us as Word documents using the TDR service. This is the digital record of the publication version of the judgment. We then create a digital surrogate of the record for publication and re-use. This is the LegalDocML version from which we create the HTML and PDF documents. Find out more about LegalDocML.

This process allows us to have consistent data about judgments and decisions. This includes:

  • Neutral citation
  • Court/chamber
  • Party names
  • Judge's name(s)
  • Date

Data about new judgments can also be consumed as a data feed.

Uniform Resource Identifier (URI)

In most cases we will use the neutral citation as the basis for how we uniquely identify judgments and decisions.

For example: https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/id/{court}[/{sub-division}/]{year}/{neutral-citation-number}

We have a dedicated team who check that all new judgments and decisions we receive have a neutral citation.

Neutral citations were adopted in 2001. Where a judgment doesn't have a neutral citation, we will create an identifier using the standard court abbreviation, the judgment date and an ordinal sequence number assigned by The National Archives (assigned in order the judgments were received).

For example: https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/id/{court}[/{sub-division}/]{year}/{judgment-date}/num/{tna-ordinal-number}

In the case where a judgment or decision has had a neutral citation issued by BAILII, we will make The National Archives' ordinal number the same as the one issued by BAILII. This means that there is 1-to-1 mapping of judgments between BAILII and Find Case Law (when the header is replaced).

Enrichment

We automatically find and hyperlink neutral citations and titles of legislation within the text of a judgment or decision.

Neutral citation links take you to the original judgment or decision if we have it as part of our collection of judgments and decisions. Find out more about our coverage of judgments and decisions.

Links to acts of legislation will take you to the legislation as it stands today. You can use the legislation 'point in time' feature to find legislation as it stood on a particular date or the original legislation passed by parliament.

Detecting changes

At present, any update to a judgment will result in it being shown in the recently updated list – this could be a minor metadata change, an enrichment with extra links, or a new version from the original submitter, with a material change to the judgment.

The judgment XML contains a content hash which will only change with material changes; you can use this to detect changes for an updated judgment. In the near future, we hope to provide a feed that only shows material changes, so you will not need to keep a list of hashes.

An example update detection workflow:

  1. Poll the recently-updated feed.
  2. Detect a new feed item using the timestamps in the feed.
  3. Fetch the XML representation of the updated document.
  4. Check the content hash against a stored value.

Data licensing arrangements

By licensing court judgments and tribunal decisions for re-use we aim to facilitate open justice and enable innovation, while protecting the proper administration of justice.

The Open Government Licence does not apply

Judgments and decisions contain personal data. This means the Open Government Licence cannot apply as it excludes personal data.

The National Archives has been working in consultation with the Ministry of Justice and the Judicial Executive Board (chaired by the Lord Chief Justice) to develop two licences that permit the re-use of judgments and decisions. Both licences are free to use:

  • The Open Justice Licence that permits re-use but excludes computational analysis. You do not need to apply for this licence.
  • A transactional licence that permits re-use including computational analysis. You will need to apply for this licence.

The personal information within judgments varies but it can be very extensive and contain distressing content.

It is for the court to decide whether reporting restrictions should apply. It is therefore possible for these decisions to be overruled in court and retrospective reporting restrictions be applied. It is important you are aware of your obligations and are compliant with the licence terms when re-using the data to avoid being in contempt of court.

Personal data and GDPR

Judgments and decisions contain personal data.

Neither type of licence is a data sharing agreement or a processing agreement for personal data. Re-users are recipients of the judgments and decisions published by The National Archives. As a Data Controller, re-users will need to satisfy themselves that they have a legal basis and valid purpose for processing personal data. The licences and application process has been carefully designed so as not to create a Data Controller/Data Processor arrangement between The National Archives and the re-users (unlike the commercial licences we issue for digital surrogates of paper records).

The courts are not subject to data protection regulation when acting in their judicial capacity. The National Archives is subject to data protection regulation. The Public Records Act 1958 provides the legal basis for our processing of personal data in judgments under UK GDPR.

It is important that you are aware of the legal obligations and that your re-use of the data is consistent with the administration of justice.

Open Justice Licence

You can use this licence to re-publish judgments or decisions on your own website. For example, in a blog post. Computational analysis of judgments including basic search indexing is not permitted under this licence.

You do not need to apply for this licence, however you should be aware of the responsibilities you have. Find out more about the terms of the Open Justice Licence.

If you are unsure that you are within the terms of the Open Justice Licence, please apply for the transactional licence.

Transactional licence

You need to apply for this licence if you intend to use court judgments and tribunal decisions in a way that is outside the terms of the Open Justice Licence. For example, to run computational analysis across judgments and decisions including basic search indexing.

You can apply for the licence at no cost by completing and submitting an application form.

The application form allows you to explain your purpose, including how you intend to re-use the data in a way that is consistent with the administration of justice.

We are assessing applications using the 'five safes' framework:

  1. Safe people
  2. Safe projects
  3. Safe data
  4. Safe settings
  5. Safe outputs

We work in accordance with The Re-use of Public Sector Information Regulation 2015. We will respond to your application within 20 working days (Section 8).

We do not discriminate against applicants (Section 13). Therefore if we have already granted a licence for the same purpose as described in your application, then it will be granted within 20 working days.

However, if your application describes an intended re-use that we have not previously considered, this may take longer. Where we are uncertain that an application is consistent with the administration of justice, we will ask for clarification from the Ministry of Justice and the Judiciary.

Once your licence is granted you can be confident that your re-use of the data is consistent with the proper administration of justice. The term of the licence is one calendar year from the date the licence has been granted.

If you are unsure, please apply.

If your application is unsuccessful, you can make a complaint against re-use of public sector information.

Transferring judgments and decisions from the courts to The National Archives

The National Archives receives a publication version of the judgment or decision. This means that any redactions or anonymization needed for publication should already have been made by the court.

The judges and clerks send judgments and decisions to The National Archives as Word documents using a service called Transfer Digital Records (TDR). Only registered users can use this service.

If you are a judge or clerk looking for information about how to transfer digital records, please email {% translate "caselaw.email" %}.

How we publish judgments and decisions

It is important that court judgments and tribunal decisions are published, unless the Courts decide there is a strong reason to withhold a judgment or decision from the public.

Judgments and decisions are sent to The National Archives ready for publication. We will never change a judgment or decision. Each judgment is published as a single web page.

Revisions

Over time we may receive several publication versions of the same judgment from the court. We will keep the digital record of all the versions, but only publish the latest version authorised by the court. Changes, refinements or corrections to the text of the currently published judgment can only be made by the court by sending a replacement version of the judgment through the TDR service.

Our due diligence

We appreciate that speedy publication is important for many of our users. The Find case law service has a small dedicated team who are experienced with legal information who review every judgment before we publish them. Judgments are received throughout the day for publication but there are peak hours (mid-morning and mid-afternoon) when we receive multiple judgments at once. Our current editorial process involves reading the entire judgment to check for any inconsistencies or errors resulting in an average publication time of three hours after receipt. Extra-long judgments can take longer to review. Judgments received after 5pm will be reviewed the next weekday morning.

Some judgments contain highly sensitive information, publication for these judgments may take up to 24 hours. Very occasionally we may need to double check the judgment with the judge. In these cases the judgment may take even longer to be published as we have to wait for confirmation that we have received the correct publication version of the judgment. Sometimes we receive judgments that we cannot publish due to technical issues e.g. embedded images in a format not suitable for web presentation. The publication of these judgments is delayed until we can resolve the issue. Improvements to the alpha service over time will lead to shorter publication times. We will not make any amendments to a judgment on the court's behalf.

If you spot an error within a judgment or decision, you can email {% translate "caselaw.email" %}.

Take down requests

Once a judgment has been published, we will only withdraw it from publication if we are instructed to do so by the judge or court.

We have developed this publishing policy in consultation with the Judicial Data Working group that is advising us.
